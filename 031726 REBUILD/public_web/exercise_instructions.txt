ID 902 — Neck CARs
Subtitle: Controlled Articular Rotation — cervical spine
Start / setup: Seated or standing upright, spine long.
Step 1: [A] Setup & Brace (SETUP) Sit or stand tall. Pack the shoulders down away from ears. Engage the muscles of the neck lightly — imagine you are trying to make a double chin. This pre-tension is what makes CARs different from a regular neck stretch: the joint moves, the surrounding tissues stay engaged.
Step 2: [B] The Half-Arc (CARS) Begin by drawing your chin toward your chest. Slowly arc the head to the right — ear toward right shoulder, continuing the arc until the ear moves toward the left shoulder, then chin returns to chest. One full half-arc takes 8–10 seconds. Move as if carving through resistance. Never tilt the head back — the arc travels chin to chest only, never through the back of the neck.
Step 3: [C] Reverse & Repeat (END-RANGE) At any point where you feel stiffness or reduced range, slow down further and breathe into that position. Do not push through pain or any sharp sensation. Reverse direction for one full arc. 2–3 rotations each direction is sufficient. If you feel dizziness at any point, stop immediately and rest.

ID 903 — Shoulder CARs

ID 904 — Wrist CARs

ID 1002 — Seated Foot Roll
Subtitle: Plantar fascia myofascial release
Start / setup: Seated in a chair, a ball or bottle under one foot.

ID 1003 — Gastroc Stretch
Subtitle: Standing calf stretch, straight knee
Start / setup: Standing, hands on a wall, one foot stepped back.
Step 1: [A] Wall Setup (SETUP) Stand facing a wall, hands flat at shoulder height for support. Step one foot back about 18–24 inches, keeping both feet pointing straight forward. The back knee stays completely straight throughout — this targets the gastrocnemius, the larger calf muscle that crosses the knee.
Step 2: [B] Lunge Forward (STRETCH) Bend the front knee and shift your weight forward until you feel a deep stretch in the calf of the back leg. Keep the back heel firmly on the floor — if it lifts, step the foot closer. Hold for 30–45 seconds. The gastroc is directly connected to the Achilles tendon, which loads the plantar fascia.
Step 3: [C] Switch & Repeat (BILATERAL) Switch sides. If one side is significantly tighter, spend an extra round there. Tight calves are among the most common contributing factors to plantar fasciitis — they increase load on the fascia with every step.

ID 1004 — Soleus Stretch
Subtitle: Bent-knee calf stretch — deeper target
Start / setup: Standing at a wall, back knee bent, rear heel down.
Step 1: [A] Wall Setup (SETUP) Same starting position as the Gastroc Stretch — one foot stepped back, hands on wall. But this time, bend the back knee slightly. This small change completely shifts the stretch from the gastrocnemius to the soleus — the deeper, shorter calf muscle that attaches below the knee.
Step 2: [B] Sink Into It (STRETCH) With the back knee bent, shift weight gently forward. The stretch will feel lower and deeper than the gastroc stretch — closer to the Achilles and heel. Keep the back heel on the floor. Hold 30–45 seconds. The soleus is often the tighter of the two calf muscles and the more overlooked.
Step 3: [C] Switch Sides (BILATERAL) Repeat on the other side. If you have one-sided plantar fasciitis, pay particular attention to the soleus on that side. This stretch is often prescribed alone for cases where standard calf stretching hasn't helped.

ID 1005 — Toe Splay
Subtitle: Intrinsic foot muscle activation
Start / setup: Seated or standing barefoot on a firm surface.
Step 1: [A] Seated or Standing (SETUP) Sit or stand barefoot on a firm surface. Place both feet flat on the floor, parallel, hip-width apart. Relax everything from the ankle down — no gripping or curling of toes. Feel the full footprint on the floor.
Step 2: [B] Spread All Toes (ACTIVATE) Actively spread all five toes as wide apart as possible without lifting the foot. Hold for 2 seconds. Most people discover they cannot independently move their toes much at all — this is normal and is exactly why this exercise exists. The abductor hallucis and the interossei are the target muscles.
Step 3: [C] Release & Repeat (CONTROL) Slowly release the spread. Pause 1 second. Repeat. If only some toes move, that is useful information — work with what you have. Over days and weeks, the range and independence improves noticeably.

ID 1006 — Arch Doming
Subtitle: Short foot exercise — intrinsic arch activation
Start / setup: Seated, one foot flat on the floor.
Step 1: [A] Setup (SETUP) Sit with one foot flat on the floor. The goal is to shorten the foot from heel to ball — creating a dome shape in the arch — without curling the toes. This is called the short foot exercise. It directly activates the flexor digitorum brevis and abductor hallucis, the primary arch-supporting muscles.
Step 2: [B] Draw Heel to Ball (ACTIVATE) Without lifting the heel or curling the toes, try to drag the ball of the foot toward the heel. The arch should visibly rise. Hold for 3 seconds. This movement is subtle and mentally demanding. If the toes curl, relax them — the arch lift must come from the intrinsic muscles, not the long toe flexors.
Step 3: [C] Release & Build (PROGRESS) Slowly release the arch. 10 reps per foot. As this becomes easier, progress to performing it standing, then standing on one leg. The short foot exercise is the single most prescribed intrinsic foot strengthening movement in sports PT and orthopedic rehabilitation.

ID 1007 — Big Toe Extension
Subtitle: Windlass mechanism mobilization
Start / setup: Seated, foot flat on the floor.
Step 1: [A] Seated Setup (SETUP) Sit with foot flat on the floor. Place two fingers lightly under the big toe. The big toe is the most mechanically important digit in walking — its extension during push-off is what tightens the plantar fascia like a winch (the windlass mechanism). Restricted big toe extension is a direct cause of arch strain.
Step 2: [B] Lift Only the Big Toe (ISOLATE) Lift only the big toe as high as possible, keeping the other four toes on the floor. Hold for 2 seconds. Then reverse — press the big toe down and lift the other four. This isolation trains the extensor hallucis and establishes independent toe control.
Step 3: [C] Assisted Extension (MOBILIZE) Using your fingers, gently assist the big toe into full extension — past where it goes on its own. Hold for 5 seconds. This passively mobilizes the first metatarsophalangeal joint and stretches the intrinsic flexors. Restricted range here correlates directly with plantar fasciitis severity.

ID 1008 — Eccentric Heel Drops
Subtitle: Alfredson protocol — straight knee
Start / setup: Standing on the edge of a step, heels hanging off the back.
Step 1: [A] Step Edge Setup (SETUP) Stand on the edge of a step or curb with both feet, heels hanging off the back. Rise up onto both forefeet to the top position. This is the Alfredson protocol — the most evidence-backed exercise for plantar fasciitis and Achilles tendinopathy. Keep the working knee straight throughout.
Step 2: [B] Single-Leg Lower (ECCENTRIC) Shift weight to one foot. Slowly lower the heel below the step level — taking a full 3 seconds to descend. You are loading the calf eccentrically: it is contracting while lengthening. This is the specific stimulus that promotes tendon and fascial remodeling. Do not rush.
Step 3: [C] Two-Leg Return (RESET) Use both feet to rise back to the top position. Switch the working leg. 15 reps each side. If this is painless, add load over time — a backpack with a book is sufficient. Mild discomfort during the movement is acceptable; sharp pain is not.

ID 1009 — Eccentric Soleus Drops
Subtitle: Bent-knee heel drops — deeper calf loading
Start / setup: Standing on the edge of a step, heels hanging off the back.
Step 1: [A] Step Edge, Knee Bent (SETUP) Same starting position as Eccentric Heel Drops — on a step edge, heels hanging. But this time, bend the working knee to approximately 30–40 degrees. This simple change shifts the load from the gastrocnemius to the soleus. The soleus attaches below the knee and is the primary load-bearing muscle of the calf during walking.
Step 2: [B] Slow Descent (ECCENTRIC) Lower the heel slowly on the bent-knee leg — 3 full seconds down. Maintain the same knee angle throughout the descent. Do not let the knee straighten during the lowering phase or you shift back to the gastrocnemius. Feel the load deep in the lower calf, just above the heel.
Step 3: [C] Two-Leg Return (RESET) Use both feet to return to the top. Switch sides. 15 reps each side. These two eccentric exercises — straight knee and bent knee — together cover the full calf-Achilles-fascia chain. Done consistently 3 days a week, clinical improvement is typically measurable within 6–8 weeks.

ID 2201 — Wall-Press TKE (Terminal Knee Extension)
Subtitle: Quad activation without compression
Start / setup: Standing, facing a wall with one foot slightly behind the other. The front knee is slightly bent.
Step 1: [A] Wall Setup (SETUP) Stand facing a wall. Place one foot slightly behind the other. The front knee is slightly bent. Keep the front heel grounded.
Step 2: [B] Press & Lock Out (ACTIVATE) Press the front heel into the floor and squeeze the quadriceps hard to 'lock out' the knee. Hold the squeeze for 2 seconds.
Step 3: [C] Release & Repeat (CONTROL) Release and repeat 10–12 times per leg. Switch sides.

ID 2202 — Straight Leg Raises
Subtitle: Quad strength without joint load
Start / setup: Lying on your back: one leg bent with foot flat, the other leg straight out.
Step 1: [A] Supine Setup (SETUP) Lie on your back. One leg bent, foot flat; the other leg extended straight.
Step 2: [B] Lift to Opposite Knee Height (STRENGTHEN) Lift the straight leg to the height of the opposite bent knee. Squeeze the quad at the top. Lower slowly.
Step 3: [C] Controlled Lower (ECCENTRIC) Lower in 3 seconds. 10–12 reps per leg.

ID 2203 — Isometric Wall Sits
Subtitle: Pain-numbing isometric hold
Start / setup: Standing: back flat against a wall, feet shoulder-width apart and 12 inches forward.
Step 1: [A] Wall Contact (SETUP) Stand with back flat against a wall. Feet shoulder-width, about 12 inches in front.
Step 2: [B] Slide Down (HOLD) Slide down into a partial squat. Hold. Isometric holds have a pain-numbing effect on joints.
Step 3: [C] Breathe (RELEASE) Hold 45 seconds. Breathe normally.

ID 2205 — Self-Resisted Clamshells

ID 2207 — Low Box Step-Ups
Subtitle: Eccentric strength, slow descent
Start / setup: Standing: facing a low step or the bottom stair.
